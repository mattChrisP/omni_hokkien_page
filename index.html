Building a speech-to-speech system for Singapore Hokkien

OmniHokkien is an ongoing effort to adapt existing omni-modal language models to understand spoken Singapore Hokkien, generate a relevant response, and speak that response aloud.

In brief

What we are working on

The current pipeline combines three separately adapted components: a Qwen2.5-Omni Thinker for understanding and response generation, a Transformer Bridge for mapping between model families, and a Qwen3-Omni Talker for speech generation.

The central technical problem is not simply translating one hidden vector space into another. Qwen2.5 and Qwen3 can represent the same answer with different token sequences and boundaries. We therefore treat the connection as a sequence-mapping problem rather than a position-wise projection.

Input
Spoken Hokkien, or English, Chinese, and Hokkien text
Output

    02 · System

    Current architecture

    The system is assembled from components adapted for different parts of the task. It should be understood as a research pipeline rather than a single model trained end to end from scratch.

    Why the bridge matters

    Linear and residual-MLP bridges transform representations one position at a time. In our experiments, that formulation did not adequately handle differences in token boundaries and sequence lengths. The selected autoregressive Transformer Bridge instead predicts the equivalent Qwen3 token sequence from Qwen2.5 answer embeddings.

    03 · Data

    Different data for different roles

    Natural drama speech supplies linguistic breadth and expression; clean synthetic speech gives tighter audio–text alignment; and conversational data more closely matches the intended interaction. The quantities below describe the current training recipe, not a single pooled corpus.

    Source Approximate size Primary role
    Common Voice nan-tw + CuriousFox NRP 40.9 hours Taiwan-Hokkien pronunciation foundation for the Talker
    Taiwan and Singapore Hokkien dramas About 129.4 hours Natural speech grounding for ASR and broader text coverage
    Clean MERaLiON synthetic speech 20.9 hours Verbatim audio–Hanji supervision
    MERaLiON adjacent-turn conversations 12,987 training pairs Question–answer adaptation and bridge training
    HumanChatBot 11,662 pairs; about 50 hours Final conversational and speech-output specialization

    04 · Findings

    What the experiments suggest so far

    1. Verbatim supervision matters. Clean audio–Hanji pairs help both speech generation and acoustic-language grounding.
    2. The cross-model mismatch is sequential. A Transformer Bridge has been more reliable than the tested linear and residual-MLP alternatives.
    3. Shallow text representations can support a working Talker. The current speech path conditions on Qwen3 hidden_states[0]; deeper conditioning remains untested.
    4. Codec alignment changes perceived speech pace. A one-frame target shift caused rushed output; correcting the alignment removed the systematic rushing we observed.

    These are findings from the current internal experiments. Formal ablations and a fixed end-to-end evaluation set are still needed.

    05 · Limitations

    What this page does not yet establish

    The samples show that the current pipeline can produce intelligible and relevant outputs in selected cases. They do not establish broad or state-of-the-art performance.

    • Evaluation is still largely based on listening and informal comparison.
    • We do not yet have a fixed public benchmark covering intelligibility, response quality, naturalness, pace, and speaker similarity.
    • Errors can propagate across the Thinker, bridge, Talker, and codec, and are not yet fully isolated.
    • Expressive variation is limited, with no explicit speaking-style control.
    • Drama speech is useful but can include noise and imperfect transcript alignment.

    Context

    Why Singapore Hokkien?

    Chinese dialect use at home in Singapore has declined across generations, while Hokkien remains important in family life, cultural memory, and communication with some older residents. This project asks what current speech-model adaptation techniques can—and cannot yet—contribute in a low-resource local language setting.
